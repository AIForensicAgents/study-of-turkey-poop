# study-of-turkey-poop
A field-friendly, educational repository documenting turkey scat (droppings, feces, scat) for biological study, teaching, and citizen-science identification.

---

## Overview
This repository is a compact, professionally oriented field guide and image-backed reference for recognizing, describing, and responsibly documenting wild turkey scat. It is intended for naturalists, wildlife biologists, educators, and curious citizen scientists who want to use non-invasive scat observations to infer diet, habitat use, and health signals without disturbing animals.

Contents:
- Field morphology notes and quick ID cues
- Habitat and diet inference tips from scat components
- Ethics and safety best-practices for fieldwork
- An annotated image gallery for training and reference
- A roadmap for future analyses (DNA, isotopes, machine learning)

---

## Why it matters
Understanding and documenting turkey droppings is more than a novelty — it supports practical conservation and research goals:

- Population and activity monitoring: scat presence helps map use patterns where sightings are rare.
- Diet and trophic ecology: what turkeys eat affects plant regeneration and invertebrate communities.
- Disease and parasite surveillance: non-invasive sampling can flag population health issues.
- Education and outreach: engaging, low-impact way to teach natural history and field methods.

Accurate, ethically collected scat records are a low-cost, high-value data source for long-term ecological thinking.

## Morphology notes
Turkey scat has recognizable characteristics that can be recorded consistently. Key morphology notes:

- Components:
  - Dark-brown/greenish central fecal mass (digested plant/insect matter)
  - White-tan urate cap or ribbon (avian waste of uric acid) — often attached or co-mingled
- Shape & size:
  - Typically tubular to slightly flattened; varies with diet and hydration
  - Length: often 2–6 cm per dropping cluster; can vary with bird size and defecation event
  - May be deposited as discrete pellets or smeared lines depending on substrate and movement
- Texture & consistency:
  - Firm, granular when diet is seed/plant-dominant; looser with high-berry or insect consumption
- Color indicators:
  - Darker, leafy-green tones suggest fresh vegetation intake
  - Red or purple tinge often indicates berries/fruit consumption
- Recording protocol (brief):
  - Photograph with scale (coin or ruler) and context (habitat, GPS if possible)
  - Note date, time, weather, substrate, nearby signs (tracks, scratching, feathers)
  - Avoid prolonged handling; use gloves and disposable instruments if sampling

## Habitat / diet clues
Scat contents and context are excellent proxies for recent diet and habitat use.

- Seeds & plant fibers: grasses, acorns, seeds from forbs suggest feeding in fields, edges, or oak-dominated woodlands.
- Berry pigments: red/purple stains and seed fragments imply berry/fruit feeding (seasonal).
- Insect parts: chitin fragments or identifiable arthropod bits indicate insectivory — more common in spring/early summer and for poults.
- Grit & small stones: indicative of gizzard grit ingestion — useful for inferring foraging behavior on hard seeds.
- Feather or hair fragments: may reflect incidental ingestion during preening or aggressive interactions.
- Habitat correlation:
  - Open fields and edges: more seed/grain remains
  - Forested understory: higher leaf/stem fragments, acorn bits
  - Wetland-adjacent areas: aquatic plant residues or sedge material

Interpreting diet from scat is probabilistic; combine scat data with habitat notes and seasonal context for robust inference.

## Ethics / Safety
This project emphasizes safe, legal, and ethical field methods. Key guidelines:

- Permits & access:
  - Obtain landowner permission and any required research or collection permits before sampling.
- Animal welfare:
  - Non-invasive observation is preferred. Avoid disturbing birds, nests, or roosts to collect scat.
- Personal safety & biosecurity:
  - Wear disposable gloves when handling scat. Use tweezers or a small shovel for transfers.
  - Avoid creating aerosols. Do not sniff or taste samples.
  - Decontaminate tools and surfaces with appropriate disinfectant (70% ethanol or bleach solutions as per institutional guidance) after use.
  - Wash hands thoroughly after fieldwork.
- Sample labeling & chain-of-custody:
  - Label samples with unique IDs, date, time, GPS, and collector initials.
  - Store samples chilled if analysis will be performed; follow institutional bio-sample shipping rules.
- Reporting:
  - If you encounter suspect disease signs (e.g., large mortality events), report to local wildlife health authorities.
- Data ethics:
  - Respect private property and sensitive species locations; redact precise GPS for public data if required to protect wildlife.

## Future work
Opportunities to expand scientific and educational value:

- DNA metabarcoding of scat to refine diet item identification (plants, invertebrates).
- Stable isotope analysis to assess long-term dietary shifts and habitat use.
- Parasite screening for community and health surveillance (with proper permits and labs).
- Build a labeled image dataset for automated classification (computer vision) to help citizen scientists identify scat in photos.
- Classroom modules and activity guides for K–12 and undergraduate field labs.
- Integration with citizen science platforms (iNaturalist, eBird)—with privacy controls for sensitive locations.

Contributions, issues, and reproducible workflows welcome via pull requests and issues. Please follow ethical guidelines above when collecting or sharing samples.
